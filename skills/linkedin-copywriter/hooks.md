# Hook Framework Reference

Hooks are the first line of a LinkedIn post. They determine whether someone clicks "see more" or keeps scrolling. Every hook must stand alone, create curiosity or tension, and be under 12 words when possible.

---

## 1. Contrarian Hook

**Description**: Challenge conventional wisdom or popular opinion. Creates cognitive dissonance that demands resolution.

**Emotional trigger**: Surprise, intellectual curiosity, slight indignation

**Examples**:
- "Most productivity advice is making you slower."
- "Your morning routine isn't the reason you're successful."
- "Stop optimizing your LinkedIn profile."

**When to use**:
- You have a genuine insight that contradicts popular opinion
- You can back up the contrarian take with evidence or experience
- The audience holds the conventional belief strongly enough to be provoked

**When NOT to use**:
- You're being contrarian for shock value without substance
- The take is only mildly different from consensus — it needs to be genuinely surprising
- The topic is sensitive and contrarianism will read as dismissive or tone-deaf

---

## 2. Story Hook

**Description**: Start mid-action or at a dramatic moment. Drops the reader into a scene without context, forcing them to read on.

**Emotional trigger**: Curiosity, empathy, narrative anticipation

**Examples**:
- "Last Tuesday, I lost my biggest client."
- "The CEO looked at me and said, 'You're done.'"
- "I was sitting in a parking lot, staring at a rejection email."

**When to use**:
- You have a real story with a genuine lesson
- The opening moment has inherent drama or tension
- The story leads somewhere useful — not just drama for drama's sake

**When NOT to use**:
- The story is fabricated or heavily embellished
- The dramatic moment is a stretch ("I opened my laptop and EVERYTHING CHANGED")
- You don't have a clear lesson or takeaway from the story

---

## 3. Data Hook

**Description**: Lead with a surprising statistic or number. Anchors the post in concrete reality and signals credibility.

**Emotional trigger**: Surprise, authority, urgency

**Examples**:
- "87% of LinkedIn posts get zero engagement."
- "The average SaaS company loses 5-7% of revenue to churn monthly."
- "I analyzed 1,000 cold emails. Only 23 got replies."

**When to use**:
- You have a compelling, specific data point (not a vague "studies show")
- The number is genuinely surprising or counterintuitive
- You can cite or explain the source

**When NOT to use**:
- The stat is well-known or obvious ("Most startups fail")
- You can't back up the number
- The data requires too much context to be meaningful in one line

---

## 4. Question Hook

**Description**: Ask something the reader can't scroll past. The best questions are ones where the reader immediately starts formulating their answer.

**Emotional trigger**: Self-reflection, curiosity, desire to respond

**Examples**:
- "What would you do with an extra $10K/month?"
- "When was the last time you said no to a client?"
- "What's the one skill you wish you'd learned 10 years ago?"

**When to use**:
- The answer reveals a pain point or aspiration
- The question is genuinely thought-provoking, not rhetorical filler
- You want to drive comments and conversation

**When NOT to use**:
- The question has an obvious answer ("Do you want more revenue?")
- It reads as manipulative or leading
- You're using it as a lazy opener because you can't think of a better hook

---

## 5. Authority Hook

**Description**: Lead with credentials, experience count, or track record. Earns the right to be heard before making the argument.

**Emotional trigger**: Trust, respect, curiosity about earned wisdom

**Examples**:
- "After 500+ sales calls, here's what I learned:"
- "I've hired 200 engineers. Here's my #1 filter."
- "10 years in B2B SaaS taught me one thing."

**When to use**:
- The experience count genuinely adds weight to the advice
- You're sharing insights that only come from volume or time
- The number is impressive enough to earn attention

**When NOT to use**:
- The credential doesn't match the topic (MBA teaching street-level hustle)
- The number is small or unimpressive for the claim being made
- It comes across as bragging rather than teaching

---

## 6. Vulnerability Hook

**Description**: Admit failure, struggle, or an uncomfortable truth. Breaks the highlight-reel pattern of most LinkedIn feeds.

**Emotional trigger**: Empathy, relatability, authenticity, relief

**Examples**:
- "I almost quit last month."
- "My first business failed in 6 months. I lost everything."
- "I've been pretending to have it figured out."

**When to use**:
- Authenticity and relatability serve the message
- The vulnerability leads to a useful insight, not just sympathy
- You're comfortable sharing this publicly and it's genuinely true

**When NOT to use**:
- The struggle is manufactured or exaggerated for engagement
- It reads as a humble brag ("I was SO stressed about my $2M launch")
- There's no lesson — just a request for emotional support

---

## 7. Pattern Interrupt

**Description**: Break expectations completely. Say something that doesn't belong on LinkedIn, or contradict the format itself.

**Emotional trigger**: Confusion, amusement, curiosity

**Examples**:
- "Stop writing LinkedIn posts."
- "This post has no advice."
- "I deleted my content calendar."

**When to use**:
- The irony or contradiction creates genuine curiosity
- You can deliver on the promise of the interrupt — the body must explain
- Your audience is sophisticated enough to appreciate meta-commentary

**When NOT to use**:
- It's just random or confusing without payoff
- The interrupt is overused in your niche (everyone's doing "hot takes")
- The body doesn't connect to the hook in a satisfying way

---

## 8. Bold Claim Hook

**Description**: Make a strong, declarative statement. Takes a firm position that demands the reader agree or disagree.

**Emotional trigger**: Agreement/disagreement, tribal identification, urgency

**Examples**:
- "Cold outreach is dead."
- "Your company doesn't have a hiring problem. It has a culture problem."
- "The best marketing strategy is a great product."

**When to use**:
- You can back up the claim with evidence or a compelling argument
- The claim is polarizing enough to generate engagement
- You genuinely believe it and can defend it in comments

**When NOT to use**:
- The claim is hyperbolic and you know it ("Email is dead" when it obviously isn't)
- You can't support it beyond the hook
- It's inflammatory without being insightful

---

## 9. How-To Hook

**Description**: Promise clear, actionable value upfront. Tells the reader exactly what they'll learn if they keep reading.

**Emotional trigger**: Desire for improvement, practical curiosity, aspiration

**Examples**:
- "How I went from 0 to 10K followers in 90 days:"
- "The exact framework I use to close 6-figure deals:"
- "How to write a cold email that actually gets replies:"

**When to use**:
- You have a clear, repeatable methodology to share
- The result is specific and desirable
- You can deliver genuine tactical value in the post body

**When NOT to use**:
- The "how-to" is vague or generic advice everyone already knows
- The result sounds too good to be true
- You're promising a framework but delivering platitudes

---

## Selection Guide

| Content Goal | Primary Hook | Secondary Hook |
|-------------|-------------|----------------|
| Educate | How-To, Data | Authority, List |
| Inspire | Story, Vulnerability | Bold Claim |
| Challenge | Contrarian, Bold Claim | Pattern Interrupt |
| Promote | How-To, Authority | Story, Data |
| Entertain | Pattern Interrupt, Story | Question |
| Start conversation | Question, Vulnerability | Contrarian |

When torn between two hooks, default to the simpler one. The best hook is the one that makes the reader's thumb stop scrolling.
