# Body Structure Patterns

Once the hook stops the scroll, the structure carries the reader to the end. Choose the pattern that best fits your content type and goal, then follow the template.

---

## 1. Story-Lesson

**Description**: Narrative arc leading to a clear takeaway. The story does the persuading; the lesson makes it actionable.

**Best for**: Personal experience, case studies, client wins, career moments

**Word count range**: 150-300 words

**Template**:
```
[Hook — drop reader into a moment]

[Setup: 2-3 lines of context. Who, where, what was at stake.]

[Conflict/tension: What went wrong, what was hard, what surprised you.]

[Resolution: What happened. Keep it concrete.]

[Lesson: 1-2 lines. The takeaway. Make it transferable.]

[Optional CTA]
```

**Example skeleton**:
```
Last Tuesday, I lost my biggest client.

They'd been with us for 3 years.
$200K in annual revenue.
I thought we were untouchable.

Then their new VP walked in and asked one question:
"What have you done for us in the last 90 days?"

I didn't have a good answer.

The lesson: Retention isn't about the relationship you built.
It's about the value you're delivering right now.

Don't coast on history. Earn the renewal every quarter.
```

**When to use**: You have a real story with a genuine turning point and a lesson that applies broadly.

## 2. List-Post

**Description**: Numbered or bulleted insights. Easy to scan, easy to save, high shareability.

**Best for**: Tactical advice, frameworks, tools, mistakes to avoid, lessons learned

**Word count range**: 100-250 words

**Template**:
```
[Hook — what the list delivers]

[Brief context: 1-2 lines framing why this matters]

1. [Item] — [1-line explanation]
2. [Item] — [1-line explanation]
3. [Item] — [1-line explanation]
...
(3-7 items. Each item: 1-2 lines max.)

[Wrap-up: 1-2 lines reinforcing the theme]

[Optional CTA]
```

**Example skeleton**:
```
After 500+ sales calls, here are 5 things I stopped doing:

1. Leading with features — Nobody cares until they feel understood.
2. Talking past the "no" — Resistance is information, not an obstacle.
3. Sending decks before calls — If they need a deck, you need a better pitch.
4. Discounting early — It trains them to always ask for less.
5. Skipping the follow-up — 80% of deals close after the 5th touch.

The common thread: Selling less, listening more.

Which one resonates most?
```

**When to use**: You have 3+ distinct, concise points that serve a single theme.

## 3. Contrarian-Take

**Description**: Challenge a widely held belief, then offer a reframe. The power comes from the evidence or logic, not just the disagreement.

**Best for**: Thought leadership, differentiation, sparking debate

**Word count range**: 100-200 words

**Template**:
```
[Hook — state the contrarian position]

[Conventional wisdom: What most people believe. 1-2 lines.]

[Why it's wrong: The flaw in the logic. 2-3 lines with evidence.]

[Reframe: Your alternative perspective. 2-3 lines.]

[Proof point: Data, example, or experience that supports your take.]

[Optional CTA — conversation starters work well here]
```

**Example skeleton**:
```
Your morning routine isn't the reason you're successful.

Everyone shares their 5am wake-up, cold plunge, journal stack.
As if the routine is the variable that matters.

It's not. The routine is the effect, not the cause.

Successful people have routines because they have clarity.
They know what matters, so they build systems around it.

Copy someone's routine without their clarity
and you just have a fancy alarm clock.

Figure out your priorities first. The routine will follow.
```

**When to use**: You have genuine insight that contradicts popular opinion and can support it.

## 4. Problem-Agitate-Solve

**Description**: Name the pain, make it vivid, then offer relief. Classic persuasion structure adapted for LinkedIn.

**Best for**: Product/service posts, lead generation, solution-oriented content

**Word count range**: 120-250 words

**Template**:
```
[Hook — name the problem or its symptom]

[Problem: Describe it in the reader's words. 2-3 lines.]

[Agitate: Make it visceral. What happens if they don't fix it? What does it cost them? 2-4 lines.]

[Solve: Your approach, framework, or solution. 2-4 lines. Be specific.]

[CTA — natural transition to the offer or next step]
```

**Example skeleton**:
```
Your best employees are about to quit.

They're not going to tell you.
They won't complain or make demands.
They'll just start interviewing.

By the time you notice, they've already checked out.
The replacement cost? 6-9 months of salary.
The knowledge they take? Irreplaceable.

Here's what we do instead:
- Monthly 1:1s focused on growth, not status updates
- Quarterly "stay interviews" — not exit interviews
- A comp review cycle that doesn't wait for a counter-offer

Retention isn't an HR problem. It's a leadership problem.
```

**When to use**: You're addressing a specific pain point your audience feels and you have a clear solution.

## 5. Before-After

**Description**: Show transformation. Paint the "before" state vividly, then contrast with the "after." The gap between them is the value.

**Best for**: Case studies, personal growth stories, product results, career pivots

**Word count range**: 120-250 words

**Template**:
```
[Hook — hint at the transformation]

[Before: The old state. Make the pain tangible. 3-4 lines.]

[Transition: The moment things changed. What happened? 1-2 lines.]

[After: The new state. Make the gain concrete. 3-4 lines.]

[How: What specifically drove the change. 2-3 lines.]

[Optional CTA]
```

**Example skeleton**:
```
6 months ago, I dreaded Mondays.

I was working 60-hour weeks.
Saying yes to every client.
Running on coffee and anxiety.
Revenue was up. I was burning out.

Then I fired my 3 worst clients.

Now:
- I work 35 hours a week
- Revenue is down 15%, profit is up 40%
- I actually like my business again

The shift wasn't about working harder.
It was about working with the right people.

Not all revenue is good revenue.
```

**When to use**: You have a clear before/after with a specific catalyst for the change.

## 6. Thread / Carousel Setup

**Description**: A teaser post that promises depth, then delivers value in chunks. Works for multi-part posts or carousel-style content within a single post.

**Best for**: Long-form educational content, step-by-step guides, comprehensive frameworks

**Word count range**: 200-400 words

**Template**:
```
[Hook — promise of comprehensive value]

[Context: Why this matters. 1-2 lines.]

[Promise: "Here's what I found:" or "Here's the breakdown:"]

[Chunk 1: Subheading or number + explanation. 2-3 lines.]

[Chunk 2: Subheading or number + explanation. 2-3 lines.]

[Chunk 3: Subheading or number + explanation. 2-3 lines.]

(3-5 chunks. Each self-contained but building on the last.)

[Summary: Tie it together. 1-2 lines.]

[CTA — save/share prompt works well here]
```

**Example skeleton**:
```
I analyzed 100 landing pages that convert above 10%.

Here's what they all have in common:

1/ The headline matches the ad.
Not "sort of" matches. Word-for-word echo.
When someone clicks, they need instant confirmation they're in the right place.

2/ The CTA is above the fold.
Not buried below testimonials.
The first action should be visible without scrolling.

3/ Social proof is specific.
Not "Trusted by thousands."
"Used by 2,847 SaaS founders" hits different.

4/ The page loads in under 2 seconds.
Every second of load time costs ~7% in conversions.
Speed is a design choice.

The pattern: Clarity beats cleverness. Every time.

Save this for your next landing page audit.
```

**When to use**: Your topic has enough depth to warrant chunked delivery and each section can stand on its own.

---

## 7. One-Liner

**Description**: The hook IS the post. A single powerful statement or observation. Maximum impact, minimum words.

**Best for**: Philosophical takes, quotable moments, mindset shifts, portfolio building

**Word count range**: 5-30 words

**Template**:
```
[The statement. That's it.]
```

**Example skeleton**:
```
The best time to start was last year.

The second best time is to stop reading LinkedIn posts and go do the work.
```

**When to use**: The idea is strong enough to stand completely alone, and adding more words would dilute it.

## Structure Selection Guide

| Content Type | Primary Structure | Secondary Structure |
|-------------|------------------|---------------------|
| Personal story | Story-Lesson | Before-After |
| Tactical advice | List-Post | Thread/Carousel |
| Hot take | Contrarian-Take | One-Liner |
| Product/service promo | Problem-Agitate-Solve | Before-After |
| Case study | Before-After | Story-Lesson |
| Educational deep-dive | Thread/Carousel | List-Post |
| Mindset / philosophy | One-Liner | Contrarian-Take |
| Engagement farming | List-Post | Question Hook + Story-Lesson |

## Formatting Rules (All Structures)

- **Short paragraphs**: 1-3 lines max. LinkedIn is a mobile-first platform.
- **White space**: Use line breaks between every thought. Dense paragraphs kill engagement.
- **No markdown in posts**: LinkedIn doesn't render bold, italic, or headers. Use line breaks and spacing for emphasis.
- **Emojis**: Use sparingly or not at all, depending on the author's voice profile. Never use them as bullet points unless the corpus shows this pattern.
- **Hashtags**: 3-5 max, at the very end, relevant to the topic. Never mid-sentence.
